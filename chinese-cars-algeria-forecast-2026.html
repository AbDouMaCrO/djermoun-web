2 · Market Evolution — From Import Drought to Chinese Dominance

Algeria's 2016–2023 import restrictions created the most under-supplied car market in North Africa: a fleet averaging 15+ years old and pent-up demand estimated at 300,000–400,000 units/year. When the market reopened, European brands — except Stellantis/Fiat, which committed to local production in Oran — hesitated. Chinese brands did not.

Fiat still leads at ~49.9% share on the strength of local Tafraoui assembly, but it is losing share (−12.4% YoY) while the Chinese wave grows underneath it: BYD jumped to #2 with 10.2% share, up 118.9% year-on-year. Haval climbed to #4 growing 140%, and Chery holds the top five. Essentially all of the market's Q1 2026 recovery is Chinese-driven.

Two shifts that matter more than raw share

  • The EV segment is being created by China, from zero. EVs hit 12.3% market share, up 160% YoY, and BYD controls roughly 83% of the segment. There is no legacy competitor in Algerian EVs — the segment is Chinese by construction.
  • Perception has flipped. In 2015, Chinese cars in Algeria meant cheap Cherys with poor resale value. In 2026, a BYD Song Plus or Geely Monjaro is a status purchase. This perception shift is durable and is China's real moat.
Forecast 2026–2030 Chinese brands collectively pass 40–45% of new-vehicle sales by 2028, driven by local assembly coming online and the EV subsidy regime. Fiat keeps the single-brand crown for 2–3 more years on price and local content, then faces a genuine fight with locally-assembled Geely and Chery product at parity pricing.

3 · Profitability: Used Imports vs. New Vehicles

These are genuinely different businesses with different ceilings.

The used-import route (< 3 years old)

Rules as of mid-2026: one import per individual every 3 years · resident individuals only (no companies) · maximum 1,600 cm³ engine displacement · petrol, hybrid or electric only — diesel is effectively blocked. The 1,600cc cap is the single most important constraint: it excludes most mid-size petrol SUVs and pushes the entire trade toward small-displacement turbos, hybrids, and EVs.

The arbitrage A petrol import carries a punitive ~50%+ tax stack while a 100% electric vehicle gets an 80% reduction on total duties and taxes, with EV purchase support (up to ~$15,000/vehicle) confirmed through end-2026. A BYD Dolphin bought in China for ~$14,000, landed and cleared for a fraction of the petrol tax burden, resells in a supply-starved market at a margin a petrol import can't touch. The 2026 finance law also removed the article 147 bis specific tax on new imported tourism cars, and Algiers is debating extending the individual import window from 3 to 5 years — both signs the state wants volume through official channels.

The ceiling of this model: it is structurally a broker/agent business (one car per person per 3 years means scaling by aggregating individual quotas — a legal gray zone), it is exposed to a single line in the annual finance law, and every plant that opens locally compresses the margin. It is a 2–4 year window: highly profitable inside it, especially in EVs, and shrinking after.

The new-vehicle route

Franchised distribution of new Chinese cars is the durable business: recurring volume, parts and service revenue (the real long-term margin in a market with an aging fleet), manufacturer support, and alignment with — rather than exposure to — government policy. Barriers: the agrément (dealer license), capital, and brand-allocation politics. Lower per-unit margin than the EV import arbitrage today, but it compounds; the import play doesn't.

Verdict Playing for 2026–2028 cash → used EV/hybrid imports (Seagull, Dolphin, Song Plus DM-i, small-displacement Tiggos under the 1,600cc cap) are the best margin in the market. Building for 2030 → position in distribution, after-sales, or parts for the brands that are localizing; that's where the value migrates the moment Geely's and Chery's lines reach volume.

4 · China's Strategic Vision — Algeria as the Western Mediterranean Base

China's approach is a template it has already run in Thailand, Brazil, and Uzbekistan: enter with exports, convert to CKD/SKD assembly under local-content pressure, then use the country as a regional export platform.

Local manufacturing is under construction, not promised

  • Geely: ~$200M plant, 50,000 units/year, production starting 2026, first model the GX3 — with stated export plans covering Africa, Latin America, and Central Asia.
  • Chery: scaling Bordj Bou Arreridj from 24,000 toward 100,000 units/year over three years; explicitly framed as a North African export hub. Local NEV production planned from 2026.
  • JAC & Changan: expanding local footprints in parallel.

Why Algeria — and not just Morocco

  1. Policy fit. Algeria requires localization for market access and offers cheap energy, industrial land, and a government actively courting non-Western industrial partners. Chinese OEMs get privileged treatment that European incumbents burned through in the 2014–2019 assembly-scandal era.
  2. Market-access geometry. Algeria borders Tunisia, Libya, Niger, Mali, and Mauritania, sits on Mediterranean shipping lanes, and is inside the African Continental Free Trade Area. Cars stamped "Made in Algeria" enter Sub-Saharan and Arab markets under preferences "Made in China" doesn't get — and they sidestep the EU/US tariff walls rising against Chinese-built EVs.
  3. The Belt-and-Road envelope. Auto plants slot into an existing Chinese infrastructure position (the El Hamdania mega-port, highways, housing). Automotive localization deepens a political relationship both states want.
The realistic caveat Phase-one "local manufacturing" means assembly at modest integration rates (~15–30% local content). True technology transfer — stamping, powertrain, battery packs — comes only if the government enforces its integration-rate schedule and export volumes materialize. Algeria's own history (Renault Oued Tlelat, the 2017-era assembly scandals) shows how localization can stall. The difference this time: Chinese OEMs actually need the export hub for tariff reasons, aligning their incentives with Algiers' in a way European assemblers' never were.
